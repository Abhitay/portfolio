The Problem

Status Quo: Equal Budget Across Segments

We allocate marketing budget uniformly across customer segments: new users, early adopters, and power users all receive the same spend-per-acquisition (SPA) treatment. But retention and LTV differ dramatically.

The Risk

  • Budget Waste: High-baseline users convert anyway; we overspend to reach them
  • Suboptimal Mix: Low-lift segments consume budget that could drive LTV growth elsewhere
  • LTV Regression: Without incremental ROI modeling, we optimize CAC but ignore lifetime value
  • Competitive Pressure: Rivals use uplift models to outbid us efficiently

The Hypothesis

By modeling incremental impact (how much revenue a user generates due to our campaign, not just total revenue), we can reallocate budget to segments where the marketing spend truly moves the needle. Result: Lower CAC + maintained or improved LTV.

The Problem with This Approach

Enterprise users convert at 52% without our marketing campaign too. They're buyers regardless. By spending $50/user to acquire them, we're paying for conversions that would have happened anyway. The incremental lift is minimal or negative.

Result: We're allocating most of our budget to low-lift segments and underspending on high-lift segments. Budget inefficiency across the board.

Approach 2: Uplift Modeling with Causal Forest (Correct)

Methodology

Type: Causal Forest (heterogeneous treatment effect estimation)
Key Innovation: Estimate per-segment incremental impact, not just aggregate conversion
Metric: Incremental LTV (marketing-driven lifetime value per dollar spent)
Approach: Train ML model on treatment/control split, estimate segment-level CATE (Conditional Average Treatment Effect)

Why Causal Forest?

Standard regression assumes the same treatment effect across all users. Causal Forest instead learns how treatment effect varies by segment‚Äîallowing us to allocate optimally.

The Analysis Steps

Step 1: Prepare Treatment/Control Data

Split Q3 cohort: ~70% exposed to marketing campaign (treatment), ~30% organic/control. Track revenue and retention outcomes for 90 days post-signup.

Step 2: Engineer Features

Use pre-treatment variables: segment (PS/FF/ER), device type, traffic source, signup day/time, initial engagement depth. No leakage from post-treatment data.

Step 3: Train Causal Forest

Fit random forest to predict heterogeneous treatment effect (LTV impact). Tree leaves represent segments with similar propensity to be lifted by marketing.

Step 4: Estimate Incremental ROI per Segment

For each segment, calculate CATE: Expected LTV(treated) - Expected LTV(control). Divide by SPA to get incremental ROI = (CATE / SPA).

Step 5: Allocate Budget Proportional to Incremental ROI

Increase budget to high-lift segments, reduce spend on low-lift. Maintain CAC constraint (e.g., CAC ‚â§ 20% of LTV for new segments).

Business Risks & Implications

Risk 1: Segment Size Instability

If price-sensitive segment becomes saturated or fatigued (from 35% ‚Üí 52% budget allocation), incremental ROI may decline, breaking our assumptions.

Risk 2: Competitive Response

Shifting budget to price-sensitive cohorts increases bidding war with competitors in that channel. CAC inflation could erode projected savings.

Risk 3: Enterprise Revenue Risk

Cutting enterprise campaign spend from $50 to $12 SPA risks perception of neglect. Could harm strategic account relationships if acquisition quality declines.

Risk 4: Model Generalization

Causal Forest was trained on Q3 data. Seasonal trends, product changes, or market shifts could invalidate segment-level treatment effects.

Model Limitations & Considerations

Causal Assumptions

  • Unconfoundedness: We assume all confounders (differences between treated and control users) are observed. Time-of-signup, device, and traffic source are measured, but user intent is not. If intent is unobserved and correlates with treatment, bias remains.
  • SUTVA (Stable Unit Treatment Value): We assume one user's campaign exposure doesn't affect another's. Network effects (e.g., referrals) could violate this.
  • Common Support: All segments must have overlap in propensity to be treated. Enterprise segment is nearly 100% targeted (high propensity), limiting comparison quality.

Model Generalization

  • Temporal Drift: Causal Forest estimates treatment effects from Q3. Seasonality (holiday campaigns, end-of-year budgets) could change segment-level lifts.
  • Channel-Specific Effects: Model pools all marketing channels (email, search, display). Segment-level ROI may vary by channel‚Äîa deeper channel-segment analysis could refine allocation further.

Data Quality Notes

  • Sample Size: 8,500 users is sufficient for overall estimation but limits granularity below 3 segments
  • Attribution Window: 90-day observation window is early for LTV. 12-month projections carry extrapolation risk

Conclusion

This case study demonstrates how causal inference transforms marketing allocation from intuition to optimization.

Naive budget allocation by conversion rate leads to overspending on high-baseline (low-increment) cohorts and underspending on responsive (high-increment) segments. Causal Forest identifies true heterogeneous treatment effects, enabling segment-level ROI optimization.

Key Lessons:

  • High absolute metrics (conversion rate) don't indicate high incremental impact (marketing ROI)
  • Incremental CAC differs from total CAC; allocate based on the former
  • Segment-level heterogeneity in treatment effects is real; average effects hide it
  • Optimization must respect constraints (LTV/CAC guardrails, segment size limits)
  • Models drift; recalibrate monthly to stay ahead of market changes
